Abstract

In recent years, Terrorist attacks have become an important threat to world piece since they became more widespread and frequent. In this project, we are using the Global Terrorism Database to chase the trajectory of terrorism in the present context with a variety of data mining techniques and statistical methods. We visualize the information (e.g. attack frequency and casualties) and look further into the prediction about the perpetrators behind the attacks, the weapons they use and type of attack. This can help us with anti-trerrorism and defense against terrorist attacks

Introduction

We describe our problems as a prediction task: using Global Terrorism Database to visualize attacks trends, to find hidden patterns and to predict unknown information.

Our contributions mainly focus on two things. For one, We do extensive research and data visualization about the characteristics of the dataset in order to prepare the basis of our prediction. After that, we tried different ways to select and encode features to improve the performance of our model. Secondly, we conduct experiments on several models including decision trees, random forests, and KNN classifiers.

Data

The Global Terrorism Database™ (GTD) is an open-source database including information on terrorist events around the world from 1970 through 2020. There are over 200,000 cases of terrorist attacks with information such as the the date and location of the incident, the weapons used and nature of the target, the number of casualties, and--when identifiable--the group or individual responsible. This data set is available from the University of maryland website and can be downloaded directly from the webpage after submitting a usage form

Visualization

  • Explanation and interpretation of each plots

Worldwide Casualties. We use Heat-map to illustrate worldwide casualties from terrorist attacks. As the figure illistrated, there are some areas that have a very high number of terrorist attacks. This is due to the presence of regional headquarters of terrorist organizations in these areas.

Attack Count. In order to visualize the changes in the number of terrorist attacks in different regions over time, we use a line chart to visualize them. Figure shows the number of terrorist attacks in 12 regions from 1970 to 2016. The advantage of this display is that it directly reflects the degree of regional instability (the more terrorist attacks, the more unstable the region.

Target Type. We used bar charts to measure the number of terrorist attacks on different targets. In addition to attacks on civilians and private property, attacks on the military, police, and government are the mainstream terrorist attacks.

Target Type and Weapon Type. We examined the choice of targets for each attack carried out by the most active terrorist groups as shown in Figure, and found that preference for target selection varies among groups. The Taliban had the highest percentage of attacks against the military, especially the police, of any terrorist group we studied. Nearly half of ISIL’s attacks have targeted private citizens and property. Similarly, groups are distinct from each other in their weapon selection when they are going to launch an attack. As a relatively cheap and readily available weapon, bombs are widely used by most terrorist groups. ISIL has a passion for bombs and has used explosives in more than half of its terrorist attacks. Terrorist attacks that use bombs tend to be suicide attacks, which explains why Iraq and Afghanistan, where ISIL is active, are the most heavily targeted areas.

  • KNN
  1. Introduce KNN:

KNN which stands for K Nearest Neighbor is a Supervised Machine Learning algorithm that classifies a new data point into the target class, depending on the features of its neighboring data points.

  1. Implementation of KNN:
  1. Data import and clean:

After importing the dataset, we can see that there are 13 predictor variables that will help us decide who did one attack.

  1. Data Normalization

Notice the “latitude” and “longitude” variables, their value scale is in 100s, whereas the rest of the variables are in single digits or 2 digits. If the data isn’t normalized it will lead to a biased outcome.

  1. Data Splicing and Training KNN Model

After cleaning the data set and formatting it, the next step is data splicing. Data splicing basically involves splitting the data set into a training set and a testing data set.

Next, we’re going to calculate the number of observations in the training data set, and the K value is the square root of the total number of observations in the data set.

  1. Results

The model trained on  all 13 features achieved the accuracy of 85.8%. However, if we remove specific location information like ‘latitude’ and ‘longitude’, we will have a worse result of 70.5%.

  1. Comment on KNN experiments:

KNN performs poorly in these prediction tasks. KNN predicts the target value by the similarities between instances and the similarity is measured by the Euclidean distance. However, about 80% of the features in the GTD dataset are categorical variables, in which distance cannot precisely reflect the in- formation. Thus KNN can only give us a good result when we introduce location information as features on this task.

Conclusion

  1. The terrorist attack is intcreasing significantly from year 1970 to 2020 especially in the regions of Middle East, North Africa, and South Asia.
  2. In addition to attacks on civilians and private property, attacks on the military, police, and government are the mainstream for terrorism.
  3. The main terrorist attack type are shooting by firearm and explosion.
  4. The decision tree regression model for predicting the terrorist group isn’t fitted well since the accuracy is 8.39%.
  5. The random forest model indicates that  increasing the number of trees seems to improve the accuracy of the prediction, but the predictions seem to be time consuming during the training.
